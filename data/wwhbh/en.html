This work is very much like Alvin Lucier's I'm Sitting in a Room, but interestingly: when creating this work, I did not yet know I'm Sitting in a Room. The original inspiration for this work came from Cathy Van Eck's series of works based on microphone and loudspeaker feedback (mainly Words, Words, Words, Empty Chairs, and What We Keep) and my interest at the time in existentialist philosophy (evoked by What We Keep, which can be traced back to the anime Black Lagoon), memory (riverrun, past Eve and Adam's..), and art practices/performances/works that leave no record.

This work was submitted as my final project for the first semester of my sophomore year; around the same time, I co-created Ecce Homo with Allen. During the final presentation, a teacher (sadly, the only one among the panel I thought could understand and accept my work) asked me: "You know your grade will be very low, right?" I replied: "I know." In truth, I didn't know — I didn't think this work was bad at all. This work was about my deepest anxieties and pain at the time: I have always been someone difficult not to notice, and I both enjoy and resent this. By then I had long been wishing and fantasizing about dying in an accident (such as a traffic collision) and being completely forgotten without a trace.

This is the artist statement I wrote on 2026.06.10. The idea that "Photography is a reality more real than reality itself, and so is recording. Recordings capture the words we never knew we said — or perhaps the words we never said at all." came from some of Susan Sontag's works (On Photography and Regarding the Pain of Others), and also from something that happened when I was experiencing We Will Have Been Here with a friend: a friend heard his own voice through the loudspeaker, including words he had never said and I had never heard.

This work is really just (why say just? Isn't this kind of concise, small-scale work what I love the most? Actually it's just (just?) a habitual, unwarranted (truly unwarranted?) self-sabotage) a microphone (preferably omnidirectional), a sufficiently long delay effect processor (100% wet/dry ratio, variable delay time, no feedback — I generally use a digital one), a loudspeaker (I usually use a full-range studio monitor, but one with its own tonal character is also fine), and a limiter (to prevent hearing damage): the audio captured by the microphone goes into the delay processor, the delay processor's output is connected to the loudspeaker, and the loudspeaker feeds back into the microphone in the physical world (I generally adjust the microphone gain and loudspeaker volume to keep the feedback level between -12 and -18 dB); the limiter is typically placed between the microphone and the delay processor, but as long as it can prevent an infinite positive feedback loop between the microphone and loudspeaker to protect the audience's hearing and audio equipment, that's sufficient.

Time is what matters most here: choose time for your space. I originally planned to set up three such systems in the school's hall, with slight differences in delay time between them, but overall around 1 hour and 30 minutes. Because one class period at our school is 1 hour and 30 minutes — this is the cycle of this space.

The cycle of time, yes, the cycle of time. This was one of the concepts I was most fascinated with at the time: standing on the beach by the sea, watching how one wave catches up with another, how one cancels another out as it retreats back into the sea — as waves of different cycles phase in and phase out, you can see the positions where they converge and cancel each other shifting accordingly: thus emerge tides of different positions, different intensities. I stood on the beach, waiting for the largest tide to wash over my feet, waiting for the smallest tide to vanish into the distance. This is the origin of my obsession with time, memory, and cycles.

Another crucial aspect is volatility. Compared to the reels of tape left behind by I'm Sitting in a Room, the data in the buffer is undeniably extremely volatile. Stored in RAM, difficult to preserve — once the system is shut down, it disappears permanently, and the original data is repeatedly overwritten in each write cycle. I need no tape to prove any of this. Only my complete erasure proves that I lived. I have always been someone difficult not to notice, and I have tried to rid myself of this trait in a destructive way.

Nevertheless, this aspect of the work's design and philosophy is undoubtedly something that can and should be criticized. The day before yesterday I watched Nuestra Tierra — even though I had always known, when these specific events were laid out before me, I couldn't help but be shocked: so many people have been disappeared. Not everyone exists in our mainstream narratives and society, and their erasure is something I cannot accept. Viewed in this light, my dreams and fantasies of being forgotten undoubtedly seem rather... privileged.

Making art is too hard.
